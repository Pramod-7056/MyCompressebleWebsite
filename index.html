Compressibility Regimes

Subsonic ($$M < 0.8$$)

Flow speeds where density changes are typically negligible. Classical incompressible aerodynamics is often applied here, though compressibility effects start at $$M \approx 0.3$$.

Transonic ($$0.8 < M < 1.2$$)

A mixed regime where both subsonic and supersonic pockets exist on the body, leading to complex shock waves, drag rise, and flutter issues.

Supersonic ($$1.2 < M < 5$$)

The flow is entirely supersonic. Disturbances cannot propagate upstream, leading to attached shock waves (oblique and normal) and expansion fans.

Hypersonic ($$M > 5$$)

Extreme speeds where high heat transfer, real-gas effects (dissociation/ionization), and thin shock layers dominate the flow physics.

Key Wind Tunnel Components

Compressible wind tunnels (especially supersonic ones) require specialized components to achieve high Mach numbers using isentropic flow principles.

  • Stagnation Chamber: Stores high-pressure air at a uniform temperature before acceleration.
  • Convergent-Divergent (CD) Nozzle: Essential for supersonic flow. The convergent section accelerates flow to $$M=1$$ at the throat, and the divergent section expands and accelerates the flow to the desired supersonic Mach number.
  • Test Section: Where the model is mounted and measurements are taken. Must have robust windows for optical flow visualization (e.g., Schlieren).
  • Diffuser: Converts the high-speed kinetic energy back into pressure energy to reduce the required power for the facility and exhaust system.
